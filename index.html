This housing needs assessment of City of Alexandria, VA. The vintage of the data is March, 2024. The objective of this report is to explore the people, place, and demand for housing in the community and the region, culminating with recommendations for future housing production that balance capturing new growth with closing the local attainability gap.

Overview

This Housing Needs Assessment will help inform Alexandria's next Housing Master Plan.

This study profiles the local community's people and housing stock, establishes an approach to meeting projected demand, and recommends the new housing mix best suited to meet local and expected need through 2040.

Alexandria's population continues to grow rapidly.

Alexandria’s household population has grown steadily since 2010. In terms of relative income levels, most of the growth has been concentrated among households earning at least 100% of AMI. Households earning less than 30% of AMI have experienced some growth as well, with the intervening income brackets largely unchanged over this period.

All income levels have grown, especially the highest income households.

Cost burden is pronounced, especially among renters.

Households are cost burdened when paying more than 30% of their income on housing costs. They are considered severely cost burdened when these costs exceed 50% of their income. For renters, this includes lease rent and utilities. For homeowners, this includes mortgage costs, property taxes, insurance, utilities, and any condo fees.

57.3% of Alexandria’s households rent, while 42.7% of Alexandria’s households own their homes (MOE: 1.2). 18,670 (44%) of Alexandria’s renting households are experiencing cost-burden, while 6,629 (21%) home-owning households are cost-burdened. Alexandria households with lower incomes are experiencing cost burden at higher rates than middle- and higher-income households. More than half of both renters and homeowners with incomes up to 60% AMI spend over 30% of their income on housing costs.

The city's housing stock includes 4,432 units that are income restricted through a variety of programs, from Low Income Housing Tax Credits (LIHTC) to HUD vouchers to public housing. In many cases, affordable pricing is protected for a limited period of time, such as LIHTC units which can expire in 15 or 30 years unless their affordability period is extended.

There are an estimated 1,877 committed affordable units in the development pipeline. If all built, these new units would bring the city’s total to 4,632 committed affordable units.

This tabulation accounts for committed affordable units with affordability controls that will expire by 2040. Given the current pipeline, the city is on track to replenish expiring units and add net-new units to the overall committed affordable housing stock.

The affordability gap is widening.

Homeownership is increasingly out of reach.

This chart compares the median listing price in Alexandria with the home value affordable to a household earning the median household income. A wider gap means higher barrier to entry for first-time homebuyers and increased risk that an existing resident might be priced out of the community if they choose or need to move to a different house.

"Affordable" is defined as monthly housing costs that do not exceed 30% of a household's income (i.e. that do not trigger cost burden). Housing costs involve more than just the monthly mortgage payment. Other costs that factor into this affordability calculus include property tax, private mortgage insurance (PMI), insurance, utilities, and condo fees.

Shortage of lower-cost and smaller units

Comparing the needs of current residents with the current housing stock reveals potential misalignments between supply and demand.

Housing mix needed to eliminate cost burden.

This chart translates the total number of cost burdened households into the mix of units they would prefer and be able to afford if they were made available. This can serve as a quantification of the community’s current attainability gap: these are the units needed to eliminate cost burden under current circumstances.

In many cases, this is an instructive but largely hypothetical analysis due to the scale of the gap and the challenge of delivering new affordable housing in large quantities. However, a community must start somewhere and there are many ways to ease attainability pressure beyond just building new units.

Little new construction in recent decades.

Most of Alexandria's housing stock was built before 1980.

The figure below tabulates the age of local housing buildings and units in terms of when they were built. Alexandria has experienced relatively steady growth and development throughout the past 80 years with particularly high activity in the 1960s and 70s. The pace has slowed somewhat in recent years. In addition, according to City assessor data, 722 residential buildings have been remodeled since 2020.
